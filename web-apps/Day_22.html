Communication

In particular, failures in communication. He got the reports about our projects on from the morning section.

The objective of a webpage is to communicate.

The arial font has an interesting history. It is a version of helvetica. Helvetica was useful for being used in advertisements.

For fonts on metal, they had to put in a lot of effort to make sure the space between characters was correct.

Computerized type-setting was a change in the game though. It was no difficulty to change the space between characters. Squeezing letters as close together as possible got popular but made it incredibly difficult to read!

For example, the letters r and n makes it look like an m. rn. Arial isn't so great for readability he states.

When you choose fonts for you pages, choose them for your readability! The only way to make sure is to actually read over it.

Remember, the point of a web page is to communicate!

Another error with Arial is what happens when you put an l next to an i? The dot on the i can be hard to tell if it's an L or an I. li.

Next, colors! Colors matter as well. We automatically get a minimum of 256 colors on a web page. And yet, people like to people colors together that blend in and make it incredibly difficult to read.

Don't dazzle your reader!

Color is eye-candy, sure. But where the heck is the text? If the text is gone, the information is gone. The objective is to get information to the reader! Not impress them with how talented the web designer is!

For example, look at the yahoo home page and the google one. For the google page, you know exactly what to do. For yahoo, you get dazzled! There's so much junk and it's confusing. Perhaps that's why their search engine went down in popularity.

As such, let's follow this: KISS
Keep
It
Simple
Silly

You want emphasis without significant distraction! Quit trying to be clever. You're trying to bring across information silly!

Another problem is videos. How long can you hold the attention of someone watching a video? For Sesame Street, you'll find that all the sketches are incredibly short. If they didn't change quickly enough, they'll lose the attention of young viewers. Not as great for adults but fantastic for little kids.

When teaching in person, it's important to be able to see if your audience is still paying attention. After about an hour, they'll start losing them. As such, lecturers do their best to be aware of this.

Apparently he watched YouTube videos to learn how to make printed circuit boards. He notes how many of those videos can be rather amateurish. These videos have a limited amount of time and they limit the info they get across. As such, they gloss over the nuances of many important details. As he notes, circuit boards have many tricky little things that come with them such as cleaning them. And yet, the video completely glosses over it! Technical literature on the other hand is sure to note this.

What this comes down to is that you have to be careful with your videos! Be as complete as you can be. Don't leave out important facts and don't just show off to the audience.
