Day 4

Created Thursday 06 February 2020

A floating point number is an approximation of a real floating point number. With pencil an paper, we know that 1/3 = 0.3333... We as humans can understand that this goes on forever. We know there is a tiny error in there. However, in a floating point number we have 0.333333. And that's it. It doesn't go on forever.

Now then, imagine this: the source code can be perfectly correct. The machine code matches the source code. But the results are still wrong! Why? The architecture of the two machines handle the floating points differently!

It is a mistake to believe that you can get accurate results in business program with floating points numbers. Computer floating point numbers are approximations of real numbers. It's hard to represent in binary. In business programs, you can't get accurate results. People get away with it because the errors are so small no one notices. At one bank, they even stated that if the error was under $5000 then ignore it. That means some programmer could introduce some error and have that excess money come to them!

70% of the countries business is processed by COBOL code. No one uses it though! It's lacking programmers. COBOL gets exact results when working with decimals (it is native to COBOL. Decimal numbers are native. In Java/C++, they use floating point numbers via binary. You can't get an exact representation for many floating point decimal numbers. Anything to the right of the decimal such as dollars and cents. It gets messier and messier. PL1 and COBOL handle decimals natively). That's why it's still used. The language itself is good but people are mistakenly believing that it's dead. Managers quit advertising today due to lack of people knowing how to use it. You'll have to contact them yourself to inquire for work with COBOL.

The big numbers classes such as in Java have to employ all sorts of rounding techniques to make them work.

Also, don't multiply large numbers by small ones!

Never ever compare floating point numbers for equality! Why is it possible to get a false on this even if float A and B represent the same number? For example, 1.000000 and 0.999999 are very close but still not equal! What's special about these two numbers? They represent exactly the same real number! That number is 1 of course. In fact, in floating pointer arithmetic +0 != -0. Remember from architecture class, you can represent 0 in both positive and negative!

Looks at this:
+12345.00000

The above has 10 digits of precision. How many digits of these are reliable? NONE! You can't trust any of them.

Many books recommend to always use double. Is this good advice? NO! What if we only have 41bytes of ram? Double takes up too much space! Floating point takes up have the space. Keeping in mind resource ussage is very important.
